## I. GLOBAL MACROECONOMIC LANDSCAPE

### 1. U.S. Inflation: The "Sticky" Problem Deepens

The week's most consequential data point was the **April CPI release (May 12)**, which printed at **0.6% MoM / 3.8% YoY** — meaningfully above consensus. Core CPI rose 0.4% MoM, and the **PPI surge of 1.4% MoM (May 13)** confirmed that pipeline price pressures are intensifying. The proximate cause across both reports was a **3.8% jump in energy costs** tied to the ongoing Iran conflict.

**Trading takeaway:** This is not transitory. The energy shock is cascading into the production pipeline and will pressure corporate margins for energy-intensive sectors. At the same time, the "higher-for-longer" rate narrative is back with force, supporting the USD and punishing long-duration equities. For FTI, however, elevated energy prices are a **structural tailwind** — they incentivize upstream capex, precisely the spending that drives subsea awards.

---

### 2. Fed Leadership Transition: Warsh Era Begins

On **May 13**, the Senate confirmed **Kevin Warsh** as the new Fed Chair, succeeding Jerome Powell. Warsh is perceived as **historically hawkish** but now faces political pressure from a pro-growth administration to ease. Crucially, Powell will remain on the board as a governor until 2028, setting up a **deeply divided FOMC**.

**Trading takeaway:** Policy uncertainty is elevated. The yield curve will be volatile as markets attempt to price Warsh's actual inclination versus political constraints. This uncertainty benefits companies with strong balance sheets and self-funded growth — a category FTI squarely falls into with its $1.3–$1.45B FCF guidance.

---

### 3. Eurozone: Stagnation with an Energy Tax

The second estimate of **Q1 Eurozone GDP confirmed +0.1% QoQ** — stagnation, not recession, but hardly robust. Germany and Ireland remain laggards, while Spain and Finland show relative resilience. The ECB is trapped: unable to cut rates due to energy-driven inflation, unable to hike due to moribund growth.

**Trading takeaway:** EUR/USD faces structural headwinds from the growth divergence with the U.S. For FTI, which has significant European and Middle Eastern operations, the weak euro is a mixed bag — it makes European-sourced engineering cheaper but can dampen regional demand for new projects.

---

### 4. China: The Surprising Bright Spot

China delivered two positive data points this week:
- **May 11:** CPI at 1.0% YoY, and critically, **PPI moved decisively positive (1.5%–1.9% YoY)**, ending the deflationary cycle.
- **May 15:** Foreign trade surged **14.9%** in the first four months, driven by EVs (+68%), lithium batteries (+43%), and wind turbines (+40%).

**Trading takeaway:** China's industrial engine is absorbing higher commodity prices, which is bullish for global demand. For FTI, Chinese industrial strength supports oil and gas demand fundamentals — though China is not a direct end-market for FTI's subsea services, it underpins the global commodity complex.

---

### 5. Geopolitics: The Iran Premium Persists

Reports through **May 15** indicated that the U.S.-Israel-Iran ceasefire is under persistent strain from drone and missile skirmishes in the Persian Gulf. The **Strait of Hormuz closure risk** — while still a tail risk — keeps a floor under oil prices.

**Trading takeaway:** High volatility in Energy (XLE) and Defense (ITA) sectors. Gold remains the preferred hedge. For FTI, **geopolitical instability in the Middle East is a double-edged sword**: it supports higher oil prices (good for capex budgets) but can delay project FIDs in the region (bad for near-term awards, especially in Surface Technologies).

---

### 6. U.S. Consumer & Industrial Divergence

- **May 14 Retail Sales:** Modest nominal gain but **real (inflation-adjusted) spending turned negative**. Consumers are trading down.
- **May 15 Industrial Production:** Remained firm, buoyed by the **AI infrastructure cycle** (data centers, semiconductors).

**Trading takeaway:** A bifurcated U.S. economy — consumers are weakening, but industrial investment is holding up. This supports industrials and energy services names like FTI while warranting caution on consumer discretionary.
